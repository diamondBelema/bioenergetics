# Bioenergetics Overview

## Bioenergetics

Bioenergetics is the study of energy flow through living systems. It encompasses how cells obtain, transform, and utilize energy to drive metabolic processes.

### Importance of Bioenergetics

- Provides an important alternative fuel source when glucose is limited
- Essential for muscular contraction, active transport, and macromolecule synthesis
- Cellular activities rely on free energy conserved in certain chemical compounds with high energy bonds

---

## ATP: The Universal Energy Currency

Adenosine triphosphate (ATP) is the principal donor of free energy in most biochemical processes. The exergonic conversion of ATP to ADP and Pi, or to AMP and PPi, is coupled to many endergonic reactions and processes.

### Structure of ATP

- Three phosphate groups linked by phosphoanhydride bonds
- Phosphoanhydride bond exists between gamma-beta and alpha-beta phosphate groups
- Phosphodiester bond (not energy rich) exists between alpha phosphate and ribose sugar

### Why ATP is Energy-Rich

- Hydrolysis generates large amount of free energy (ΔG° = -7.3 kcal/mol or -30.5 kJ/mol)
- Products have lower energy state than the parent compound
- Energy is released through coupling to cellular processes

### ATP Utilization

- Direct hydrolysis generates energy for muscular contraction in muscle cells
- Energy transferred through phosphoryl, pyrophosphoryl, or adenyl groups to substrates or enzymes

## High Energy Compounds

Cells require large amounts of free energy for important activities. Cellular activities rely on free energy conserved in chemical compounds with high energy bonds.

### Types of High Energy Bonds

#### 1. Phosphoanhydride Bond

- Formed between two molecules of phosphoric acid
- Hydrolysis generates ~30.5 kJ/mol
- Found in ATP (2 phosphoanhydride bonds), all tri- and diphosphates of purine and pyrimidine nucleosides

#### 2. Enolphosphate Bond

- Formed when phosphate group is attached to hydroxy group bounded to carbon with a double bond
- Energetically the richest bond in hydrolysis (~61 kJ/mol)
- Found in phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) produced during glycolysis
- Energy used to phosphorylate ADP into ATP via pyruvate kinase

#### 3. Acyl Phosphate Bond

- Formed by reaction of carboxylic acid with phosphate group
- Hydrolysis generates ~49 kJ/mol
- Found in 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate
- Hydrolysis coupled to phosphorylation of ADP into ATP in glycolysis

#### 4. Guanidine Phosphate Bond

- Formed when phosphate group is attached to guanidine group
- Releases ~43 kJ/mol upon hydrolysis
- Found in phosphocreatine (instantaneous energy generation in skeletal muscles)
- Also found in arginine phosphate
- These compounds are known as phosphagens

#### 5. Thioester Bond

- Formed between sulfur atom and carbon atom
- Occurs when sulfur replaces oxygen in a regular ester bond
- Found in acetyl-CoA (rest of carboxylic acid attached to coenzyme A)
- Hydrolysis liberates ~41 kJ/mol

## Biological Oxidation and Reduction

The flow of electrons in oxidation-reduction reactions is directly or indirectly responsible for all work done by living organisms.

### Basic Principles

- Biological oxidation-reduction involves two half reactions, each with a characteristic E°'
- Loss of electrons by chemical species in one half (oxidation)
- Gain of electrons by another species in the other half (reduction)
- Reducing agent donates electrons, hydride ion, or proton to acceptor molecule
- After action, reducing agent becomes oxidized, acceptor becomes reduced

### Standard Reduction Potential (E°')

- Tendency of half cell reaction to donate electrons measured in volts
- Higher potential to donate electrons = more negative reduction potential
- E°' defines E° value at pH 7 and 25°C with 1M concentration of each redox species
- ΔE°' = E°' of electron acceptor minus E°' of electron donor

### Relationship Between Free Energy and Reduction Potential

ΔG°' = -nFΔE°'

Where:
- n = number of electrons transferred
- F = Faraday constant
- R = Gas constant
- T = Temperature

### Electron Flow Direction

- Electrons flow from more negative to more positive E°'
- Flow releases energy used to pump protons at Complexes I, III, and IV
- Oxygen (O₂) is the ultimate electron acceptor with highest reduction potential (+0.82 V)

## Electron Carriers

Oxidation reactions in living cells require specialized electron carriers.

### Types of Electron Carriers

#### Water-Soluble Coenzymes
- NAD⁺ (Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide)
- NADP⁺ (Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate)
- FMN (Flavin mononucleotide)
- FAD (Flavin adenine dinucleotide)

#### Lipid-Soluble Carriers
- Ubiquinone (Coenzyme Q)
- Plastoquinones

#### Tightly Bound Prosthetic Groups
- Iron-sulfur proteins
- Cytochromes
- Copper proteins

### NAD⁺/NADP⁺

- Accept hydride ion (two electrons and one proton) from oxidizable substrate
- NAD⁺ functions in oxidation (catabolic processes)
- NADPH functions in reduction (anabolic processes)
- Over 200 enzymes catalyze reactions utilizing these carriers

### Flavin Nucleotides (FMN/FAD)

- Derived from riboflavin
- Tightly bound to associated enzymes (flavoproteins)
- Accept two hydrogen atoms (two electrons and two protons)
- Both appear in flavin ring system (isoalloxazine ring)
- When accepting only one hydrogen atom, form semiquinone (stable free radical)

### Coenzyme Q (Ubiquinone)

- Benzoquinone derivative
- Localized at mitochondrial respiratory chain
- Directly involved in electron transport and coupling with oxidative phosphorylation
- Contains long isoprenoid tail enabling rapid membrane diffusion

### Iron-Sulfur Proteins

- Contain unique form of nonheme iron (Fe-S)
- Iron bound to sulfur in elemental form or to thiol in cysteine side chain
- Participate in electron transport through oxidation-reduction
- Common forms: [2Fe-2S], [3Fe-4S], [4Fe-4S]
- Involved in respiration, metabolic processes, protein translation, DNA synthesis and repair

## Energy Pathways

### Aerobic Respiration

- Complete oxidation of glucose to CO₂ and H₂O
- Produces ~30-32 ATP per glucose
- Requires oxygen as final electron acceptor
- Occurs in cytoplasm (glycolysis) and mitochondria (TCA cycle, ETC)

### Anaerobic Glycolysis

- Incomplete oxidation of glucose to lactate
- Produces 2 ATP per glucose
- Does not require oxygen
- Important for rapid energy production in muscles

### Gluconeogenesis

- Synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors
- Requires energy input (6 ATP per glucose)
- Important for maintaining blood glucose levels

### Glycogenolysis

- Breakdown of glycogen to glucose-1-phosphate
- Important for rapid glucose mobilization
- Occurs in liver and muscle

### Pentose Phosphate Pathway

- Alternative pathway for glucose oxidation
- Produces NADPH for reductive biosynthesis
- Generates ribose-5-phosphate for nucleotide synthesis

### Fatty Acid Oxidation (β-Oxidation)

- Sequential removal of 2-carbon units from fatty acids
- Produces acetyl-CoA, NADH, and FADH₂
- Major energy source during fasting

### Ketogenesis

- Production of ketone bodies from acetyl-CoA
- Important alternative fuel source when glucose is limited
- Occurs mainly in liver

### Protein Catabolism

- Amino acids can be used for energy
- Carbon skeletons enter metabolic pathways at various points
- Amino groups disposed of via urea cycle

### Phosphagen System

- Uses creatine phosphate to rapidly regenerate ATP
- Provides instant energy for short intense bursts
- Important for skeletal muscle contraction

---

## Metabolic Integration

### Cori Cycle

- Links anaerobic glycolysis in muscle with gluconeogenesis in liver
- Muscle produces lactate → transported to liver → converted to glucose → returned to muscle
- Net energy-consuming process

### Glucose-Alanine Cycle

- Similar to Cori cycle but uses alanine instead of lactate
- Transports amino nitrogen from muscle to liver
- Alanine converted to pyruvate in liver for gluconeogenesis

### Hormonal Regulation

- **Insulin**: Promotes glucose uptake, glycolysis, glycogen synthesis
- **Glucagon**: Promotes glycogenolysis, gluconeogenesis
- **Epinephrine**: Promotes glycogenolysis, lipolysis
- **Cortisol**: Promotes gluconeogenesis, protein catabolism

## Clinical Correlations

### Metabolic Acidosis

- Accumulation of lactic acid or ketoacids
- Results from impaired oxidative phosphorylation
- Can lead to respiratory compensation

### Obesity

- Imbalance between energy intake and expenditure
- Involves dysregulation of lipid metabolism
- Therapeutic targets include AMPK pathway

### Exercise Physiology

- Different energy systems used during exercise
- Phosphagen system (0-10 seconds)
- Anaerobic glycolysis (10 seconds - 2 minutes)
- Aerobic metabolism (>2 minutes)

### Cancer Metabolism (Warburg Effect)

- Cancer cells preferentially use glycolysis even in presence of oxygen
- Known as aerobic glycolysis
- Provides biosynthetic precursors for rapidly dividing cells
