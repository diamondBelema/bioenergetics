# Enzymes, Coenzymes and Bioenergetics

## What Are Enzymes?

Enzymes are biological catalysts (largely proteins and sometimes RNA molecules) that speed up chemical reactions in the body without being consumed in the process.

### Key Characteristics

- Versatile, highly efficient and specific biological catalysts
- Can recognize specific structures in the presence of similar structures
- Catalyzed reactions do not usually generate unwanted side reactions
- Living systems require enzymes to accelerate and control rates of important biochemical reactions

### What If There Were No Enzymes?

Without enzymes, life as we know it would not be possible:

- Metabolic reactions would occur too slowly (glucose breakdown might take years)
- ATP synthesis in mitochondria would be significantly impaired
- Digestion would not proceed (amylase, lipase, proteases required)
- Accumulation of toxic substances (catalase, superoxide dismutase needed)
- Impaired DNA and RNA function (DNA polymerase, RNA polymerase essential)
- Collapse of immune system

## Characteristics of Enzyme-Catalyzed Reactions

### Substrate Specificity

Enzymes usually have specific substrates which they can interact with and facilitate conversion to product. The ability to select specific substrates from chemically similar compounds is known as specificity.

#### Types of Specificity

1. **Absolute Specificity**: Enzyme acts on only one specific substrate
   - Example: Urease catalyzes hydrolysis of urea into CO₂ and ammonia

2. **Group Specificity**: Enzymes act on substrates with particular functional groups
   - Example: Alcohol dehydrogenase acts on alcohols, catalyzing oxidation to aldehydes or ketones

3. **Bond Specificity**: Enzymes recognize and act on specific types of bonds regardless of rest of molecule
   - Example: Lipase hydrolyzes ester bonds in triglycerides

4. **Stereochemical Specificity**: Enzymes act only on particular isomers
   - Example: L-Amino acid oxidase specifically acts on L-isomers of amino acids

## Enzyme-Substrate Interactions

### The Active Site

- Substrate binds specifically to active site on enzyme
- Active site is very small relative to overall protein size (cleft or pocket)
- Active site binds and orients cofactors or prosthetic groups

### Binding Models

#### Lock and Key Model (Emil Fischer, 1894)

- Shape of substrate and active site fit together like key into lock
- Both shapes are rigid and fixed
- Perfectly complement each other when brought together (geometric complementarity)

#### Induced Fit Model (Daniel Koshland, 1958)

- Binding of substrate induces conformational change in active site
- Different enzymes show features of both models
- Some complementarity and some conformational change

## Mechanism of Enzyme Catalysis

### Basic Steps

1. Substrate approaches active site
2. Enzyme-substrate complex forms
3. Substrate transformed into products
4. Products released
5. Enzyme recycled

### Reaction Scheme

E + S → [ES] → [EP] → E + P

Where [EP] is unstable

### Transition State

- Substrates need to be activated for reaction to occur
- Maximum energy level occurs at transition state
- Rate of reaction depends on how easily reactants reach transition state
- Enzymes stabilize transition state, lowering activation energy

### Energy Barrier

- Every reaction system requires activation energy to be overcome
- Enzyme lowers required activation energy
- Facilitates easier and faster reaction

## Enzyme Catalytic Strategies

### 1. General Acid-Base Catalysis

- Functional group on protein donates proton (acid) or accepts proton (base)
- Stabilizes transition state during enzyme-catalyzed reaction

**General Acid Catalysis:**
- Protonated amino acid residue side chain donates proton to substrate
- Stabilizes developing negative charge or makes leaving group easier to cleave

**General Base Catalysis:**
- Unprotonated amino acid residue side chain abstracts proton from substrate
- Increases nucleophilicity

**Key Residues:**
- Histidine most versatile (pKa near physiological pH ~6.0)
- Glutamate, aspartate, lysine, and cysteine also frequent participants

### 2. Covalent Catalysis

- Substrate covalently linked to amino acid side chain at active site
- Two-step pathway:
  1. Nucleophilic group on enzyme attacks electrophilic center on substrate
  2. Intermediate undergoes subsequent reaction to regenerate free enzyme

- Replaces single high-energy transition state with two lower-energy transition states
- Active-site nucleophiles: cysteine (sulfhydryl), serine (hydroxyl), lysine (amine)
- Coenzymes like TPP or PLP also utilized for temporary covalent linkages

### 3. Metal-Ion Catalysis

**Charge Stabilization:**
- Metal ions shield, polarize or neutralize charges
- Facilitates reaction

**Water Activation:**
- Electrophilic nature polarizes water molecule
- Forms reactive hydroxide ion
- Example: Zn²⁺ in carbonic anhydrase reaction

**Oxidation-Reduction:**
- Transition metals (Fe, Cu) reversibly change oxidation state
- Participate in electron transfer reactions

### 4. Catalysis by Approximation

- Enzyme forces substrates to bind in appropriate orientation
- Uses hydrogen bonds and ionic interactions
- Places reactive groups in correct position for reaction

### 5. Cofactor Catalysis

- Required cofactor forms covalent bond with substrate during reaction
- Example: Pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B₆) in amino acid metabolism

## Factors Affecting Enzyme Action

### 1. Temperature

- Increases reaction rates by increasing thermal energy of substrate
- At high temperatures, enzyme denatures and loses native fold and activity
- Each enzyme has characteristic optimum temperature
- Many mammalian enzymes active around 37°C

### 2. pH

- Each enzyme has optimum pH at which reaction rate is maximum
- Small deviations lead to decreased activity
- Changes ionization of groups at active site

### 3. Substrate Concentration

- Rate increases with substrate concentration until saturation
- At saturation, all active sites are occupied (Vmax)

### 4. Cofactors and Coenzymes

- Many enzymes require small, nonprotein units for activity
- Can be inorganic ions (Zn²⁺, Fe²⁺) or organic molecules (coenzymes)

### 5. Inhibitors/Activators

- Inhibitors decrease enzyme activity
- Activators increase enzyme activity

## Cofactors, Coenzymes, and Prosthetic Groups

### Definitions

- **Cofactor**: Nonprotein unit required for enzyme activity
- **Coenzyme**: Complex organic molecule cofactor
- **Prosthetic group**: Cofactor covalently attached to enzyme
- **Holoenzyme**: Complete catalytically active enzyme with cofactor
- **Apoenzyme**: Protein part of enzyme without cofactor

**Holoenzyme = Apoenzyme + Cofactor**

### Coenzyme Function

- Some coenzymes (like NAD⁺) are bound and released during catalytic cycle
- Function as cosubstrates
- Many derived from vitamin precursors
- Deficiency leads to disease

## Enzyme Classification

### International Union of Biochemistry and Molecular Biology (IUBMB) System

Seven broad classes based on type of reaction catalyzed:

| Class | Name | Reaction Type | Example |
|-------|------|---------------|---------|
| EC 1 | Oxidoreductases | Oxidation-reduction | Lactate dehydrogenase |
| EC 2 | Transferases | Transfer functional groups | Hexokinase |
| EC 3 | Hydrolases | Hydrolysis | Lipases, proteases |
| EC 4 | Lyases | Addition/removal to/from double bonds | Pyruvate decarboxylase |
| EC 5 | Isomerases | Rearrangement of atoms | Phosphoglucomutase |
| EC 6 | Ligases | Joining molecules using ATP | DNA ligase |
| EC 7 | Translocases | Translocation across membranes | Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase |

### EC Number Format

EC [class].[subclass].[sub-subclass].[serial number]

Example: EC 3.4.11.4 (tripeptide aminopeptidases)
- EC 3: Hydrolases
- EC 3.4: Hydrolases acting on peptide bonds
- EC 3.4.11: Hydrolases cleaving amino-terminal amino acid
- EC 3.4.11.4: Hydrolases cleaving amino-terminal from tripeptide

## Enzyme Kinetics

### Michaelis-Menten Kinetics

- Describes relationship between reaction velocity and substrate concentration
- V = Vmax[S] / (Km + [S])

### Key Parameters

- **Vmax**: Maximum reaction velocity
- **Km (Michaelis constant)**: Substrate concentration at half Vmax
  - Low Km = high affinity
  - High Km = low affinity

### Lineweaver-Burk Plot

- Double reciprocal plot (1/V vs 1/[S])
- Linear transformation of Michaelis-Menten equation
- Used to determine Km and Vmax

### Enzyme Inhibition

#### Competitive Inhibition

- Inhibitor competes with substrate for active site
- Increases apparent Km
- Does not affect Vmax
- Can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration

#### Non-competitive Inhibition

- Inhibitor binds to allosteric site
- Decreases Vmax
- Does not affect Km
- Cannot be overcome by increasing substrate concentration

#### Uncompetitive Inhibition

- Inhibitor binds only to enzyme-substrate complex
- Decreases both Km and Vmax
- Cannot be overcome by increasing substrate concentration

## Clinical Correlations

### Enzyme Deficiencies

- Can lead to metabolic disorders
- Examples: Phenylketonuria (PKU), Galactosemia

### Enzymes as Diagnostic Markers

- Elevated levels in blood indicate tissue damage
- Troponin for myocardial infarction
- ALT/AST for liver damage
- Amylase/lipase for pancreatitis

### Drug Design

- Many drugs are enzyme inhibitors
- Statins inhibit HMG-CoA reductase
- ACE inhibitors for hypertension
- Protease inhibitors for HIV
